\section{Aim}
The aim is to build a working mobile charger. The circuit must output \SI{5}{\volt} DC to charge a mobile phone after taking \SI{230}{\volt} AC as input.

\section{Materials Required}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Breadboard
	\item Printed circuit board
	\item 12-0-12 Tranformer
	\item 4 diodes
	\item \SI{100}{\micro\farad} Capacitor
	\item 7805 Regulator
	\item Several electrical wires
	\item Soldering iron and wire
	\item Multimeter
	\item Cathode-ray oscilloscope
	\item Output pin
	\item USB cable
	\item Mobile phone
\end{itemize}

\section{Circuit Explanation}

\subsection{Transformer}
	 The transformer steps down the \SI{230}{\volt} AC main supply to \SI{12}{\volt} AC. Note that these are RMS voltages. The peak voltage will thus be $12\sqrt{2} \approx \SI{20}{\volt}$. The transformed voltage is given by
	\begin{align}
		v(t) = \SI[parse-numbers=false]{12\sqrt{2}\sin(100\pi t + \phi)}{\volt}
	\end{align}
	\begin{figure}[!ht]
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{./figs/transformer.jpeg}
		\caption{CRO output after transformer}
		\label{fig-transformer}	
	\end{figure}

\subsection{Rectifier}
The full-wave rectifier is realized using four Si diodes arranged in a
bridge form as shown in Circuit Diagram. The output waveform at this
stage is a DC 12 V, 50 Hz rectified sinusoid.

\begin{figure}[!ht]
	\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/rectifier.jpeg}
	\caption{Output half-wave rectified waveform across diode .}
	\label{fig:rectifier}
	
\end{figure}

\subsection{Filter}
The 100 $mu$F
capacitor acts as a first order analog low pass filter. It filters
around the zero frequency DC component and partially eliminates the
even harmonics associated with the rectified sinusoid. The output 
waveform at this stage consists of the constant DC component. Note
there is no gain and hence we require a regulator to obtain the
required DC 5V supply.

\begin{figure}[!ht]
	\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/filter.jpeg}
	\caption{Output DC waveform at filter stage .}
	\label{fig:filter}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Regulator}
The regulator used in this circuit is a 7805 regulator, which outputs
a constant DC supply of 5 V with very little ripple through a 
feedback mechanism. The regulator will work because the DC component 
associated with the rectified waveform is
\begin{align}
    V_{DC} = \frac{2V_p}{\pi} = \frac{2\times12\sqrt{2}}{\pi} > \SI{5}{\V}
\end{align}
where $V_p$ is the peak voltage. Thus, we obtain an almost
constant supply of 5 V DC to charge a mobile phone.

\begin{figure}[!ht]
	\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/regulator.jpeg}
	\caption{Output DC waveform at regulator stage.}
	\label{fig:regulator_dc}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[!ht]
	\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/ripple.jpeg}
	\caption{Output AC ripple at regulator stage .}
	\label{fig:regulator_ac}
\end{figure}

\section{Results}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Peak voltage after transformer and rectifier stage, 
        $V_p = \SI[parse-numbers=false]{18}{\V}$.
    \item DC component after filter stage, 
        $V_{DC} = \SI[parse-numbers=false]{18}{\V}$.
    \item DC component after regulator stage, $V_{DC}' = \SI{5}{\V}$.
    \item Ripple after regulator stage, $\epsilon = \SI{5}{\milli\V}$.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Learning Outcomes}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Working and implementation of a low-pass analog filter.
    \item Use of regulator to help realise an analog low-pass filter with
        very low cutoff frequency.
    \item Use of lab equipment such as solder, oscilloscope, breadboard, PCB, etc.
        to realize a circuit.
\end{enumerate}
